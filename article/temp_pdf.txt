ABSTRACT
Accurate reconstruction of missing well-log data is essential for subsurface characterization and reservoir 
modeling but remains challenging under conditions of stratigraphic heterogeneity and multi-log incompleteness. 
This study introduces a deep learning framework that enhance missing log prediction by embedding lithological 
information as a contextual constraint. The proposed framework integrates a Conditional Variational Autoen -
coder (CVAE) with a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)-based lithology predictor, namely the Iterative Lithology- 
Constrained Hybrid CVAE – LSTM Network (ILCH-Net), in an iterative refinement process. The model was trained 
and validated on 45,809 samples from six wells in the Volve oil field, Norwegian North Sea, comprising five 
commonly acquired logs (GR, RHOB, NPHI, DTC, DTS) across three lithologies (claystone, sandstone, limestone). 
Quantitative evaluation demonstrates that ILCH-Net surpasses baseline approaches (Autoencoder, Iteratively 
refined autoencoder, LSTM), achieving lower root mean squared error (10.43 vs. 12.84 for LSTM) and improved 
distributional similarity (median Kolmogorov – Smirnov statistic of 0.15 with an interquartile range of 0.09 across 
the six test wells). Lithology-specific analysis further shows that reconstruction accuracy is highest for limestone 
and claystone, reflecting their distinct well-log responses, while sandstone exhibits greater variability due to 
depth-dependent compaction effects. These results confirm that lithological constraints not only enhance ac -
curacy but also reduce inter-well variability, thereby yielding geologically consistent reconstructions. By 
embedding geological priors within a data-driven framework, ILCH-Net provides a robust and scalable solution 
for applications in reservoir characterization, digital rock modeling, and geomechanical analysis where incom -
plete or irregular logs are prevalent.
1. Introduction
Well-logging is routinely employed to obtain subsurface information 
essential for characterizing the petrophysical properties of geological 
formations ( Wang and Cao, 2023 ; Khan and Kirmani, 2024 ). Among 
various logging techniques, sonic logging — which measures compres -
sional slowness (DTC) and shear slowness (DTS) — is particularly 
important for interpreting complex and heterogeneous subsurface for -
mations ( Elkatantny et al., 2018 ; Wang et al., 2024 ). DTC and DTS are 
widely used to evaluate mechanical properties such as formation 
strength, pore pressure, and fluid saturation while accounting for sub -
surface variability ( Sulaimon and Teng, 2020 ; Eyinla et al., 2021 ; 
Najafi-Silab et al., 2023 ).
Despite its usefulness, DTS acquisition remains costly and technically 
challenging due to poor signal transmission in unconsolidated or 
fractured formations, attenuation in clay-rich or fluid-saturated zones, 
and limited accessibility in deviated wells ( Onalo et al., 2018 ; Khatibi 
and Aghajanpour, 2020 ). In addition, issues such as incomplete logging, 
degraded data quality, and frequent data gaps — combined with high 
acquisition and processing costs — further restrict the reliable applica -
tion of shear-wave slowness in subsurface interpretation and modeling, 
as these deficiencies propagate uncertainty into petrophysical and geo -
mechanical analyses ( Boulassel et al., 2025 ).
Traditional DTS prediction methods rely on physically based models, 
which explore the relationships between shear-wave velocity and rock 
parameters such as clay content, mineral moduli, and pore aspect ratios 
( J ø rstad et al., 1999 ). However, these models tend to oversimplify 
geological structures. Alternatively, empirical models build statistical 
correlations from accessible well-log data ( Ameen et al., 2009 ; Olor -
untobi and Butt, 2020 ), but their performance often suffers from the

To address these challenges, machine-learning models have emerged 
as powerful tools for predicting DTS from existing well-log data. Recent 
work has further demonstrated that machine-learning frameworks can 
reliably predict dynamic shear-wave slowness from incomplete or noisy 
datasets, thereby overcoming the acquisition and quality limitations of 
conventional physics-based approaches ( Boulassel et al., 2025 ). 
Methods such as artificial neural networks (ANNs) and support vector 
machines have shown superior accuracy compared to empirical models 
( Bagheripour et al., 2015 ; Onalo et al., 2018 ). More advanced models, 
including recurrent neural networks and their variants like long 
short-term memory (LSTM) and bidirectional LSTM (bi-LSTM), have 
been employed to capture temporal dependencies and nonlinearities. 
For example, Zhang et al. (2020) applied LSTM networks to predict 
shear-wave velocity in carbonate reservoirs, and Feng et al. (2024)
further improved these predictions using a hybrid model combining 1D 
convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and bi-LSTM with attention 
mechanisms.
While these discriminative models highlight the potential of data- 
driven methods, their scope has been predominantly limited to recov -
ering DTS (or DTC) under the assumption that all other well-logs are 
fully available. In reality, however, incomplete logging does not only 
affect DTS but also other essential well-logs such as gamma ray (GR), 
bulk density (RHOB), and neutron porosity (NPHI). Technical and 
operational constraints often result in multi-log missing scenarios, 
where several logs are simultaneously absent or degraded ( Ng and 
Jordan, 2001 ; Lu et al., 2021 ). Such cases substantially reduce the 
reliability of reservoir characterization, geomechanical modeling, and 
digital rock analysis, yet they have been largely overlooked in previous 
studies that focus narrowly on DTS recovery.
In response, increasing attention has been given to generative ap -
proaches capable of handling missing data by learning the joint distri -
bution of input variables ( Camio et al., 2019 ; Mattei and Frellsen, 2019 ). 
For instance, Guo et al. (2023) proposed a GAN-based framework that 
integrates LSTM and CNN to reconstruct DTS data in shale-gas reser -
voirs. However, GANs often suffer from instability and mode collapse 
( Tang, 2020 ; Saxena and Cao, 2021 ; Chakraborty et al., 2024 ). Condi -
tional variational autoencoders (CVAEs) provide a more stable alter -
native by modeling data in a probabilistic latent space ( Bao et al., 2017 ). 
Jeong et al. (2021) , for instance, demonstrated that CVAEs can effec -
tively reconstruct DTS values while capturing uncertainty due to vari -
ability in well-log measurements. Nonetheless, most generative 
approaches have not addressed the complexities of field-scale scenarios 
involving multiple missing inputs. Moreover, both discriminative and 
generative methods often lack geological consistency, as they depend 
primarily on statistical correlations rather than domain knowledge, 
which can result in geologically implausible predictions.
To enhance geological realism in predictions, several studies have 
incorporated lithological data—such as lithofacies sequences—into 
modeling workflows. Core-derived lithology data has been shown to 
reflect trends in well-log responses, thereby improving prediction ac -
curacy ( Jeong et al., 2014 ; Al-Mudhafar, 2019 ; Ren et al., 2019 , 2023 ; 
Liu et al., 2021 ; Hou et al., 2024 ). For instance, Al-Mudhafar (2019)
demonstrated that lithology integration enhances permeability predic -
tion, while Ren et al. (2019 , 2023) developed models that combine 
facies and ANN-based time-series regressions for well-log reconstruc -
tion. These findings highlight the importance of lithological constraints 
in guiding models toward geologically plausible predictions.
Building on these insights, the present study proposes a hybrid deep 
learning framework that extends beyond conventional DTS prediction to 
reconstruct multiple well-log types simultaneously. The proposed Iter -
ative Lithology-Constrained Hybrid CVAE–LSTM (ILCH-Net) combines a 
CVAE with a LSTM-based lithology predictor within an iterative feed -
back process. By embedding lithological constraints, the model achieves 
geologically consistent and robust reconstructions of DTS as well as 
other missing logs (GR, RHOB, NPHI, and DTC).
To validate the proposed framework, we applied ILCH-Net to real 
well-log and lithology data from the Volve oil field in the Norwegian 
North Sea. A preliminary Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was 
conducted to assess the relationships between lithological classes and 
well-log responses, providing a rationale for embedding lithological 
constraints. The model was evaluated under both single- and dual-log 
missing scenarios and benchmarked against baseline approaches, 
including a standard Autoencoder (AE), an iteratively refined Autoen -
coder (Iter-AE), and an LSTM network. Across all test wells, ILCH-Net 
demonstrated high accuracy and robustness, with lithological con -
straints yielding more geologically consistent reconstructions.
2. Material and methods
2.1. Material
Well-log data and lithological information from the Volve oil field, 
located in the central region of the Norwegian North Sea, approximately 
5 km north of the Sleipner Øst field, are utilized to validate ILCH-Net 
( Fig. 1 ). The well-logs used in this study are acquired from six wells 
(W1: 15/9-F-1 A; W2: 15/9-F-1 B; W3: 15/9-F-11 A; W4: 15/9-F-11 T2; 
W5: 15/9-F-14; and W6: 15/9-F-4), and include GR, NPHI, RHOB, DTC, 
and DTS logs. An example of the well-log data and lithological sequence 
from well #15/9-F-11 T2 is presented in Fig. 2 . This study considers five 
lithological types—sandstone, claystone, limestone, marl, and marl/ 
limestone—based on the classification results reported by Talinga and 
Reine (2021) . Statistics for each well-log corresponding to lithologies in 
the study area are summarized in Table S1 of the Supplementary Ma -
terial I. More detailed information about the Volve oil field can be found 
in Equinor (2018) .
2.2. Method
The proposed methodology reconstructs missing well-log data under 
realistic multi-log scenarios by combining generative modeling with 
lithological constraints. The workflow consists of four stages: (1) pre -
processing and statistical evaluation of well-log and lithology data; (2) 
construction of the ILCH-Net framework by integrating a CVAE with an 
LSTM-based lithology predictor for iterative refinement; (3) iterative 
training with feedback cycles to update lithological constraints; and (4) 
evaluation through cross-validation and benchmarking against baseline 
models. This systematic workflow provides a robust basis for validating 
the effectiveness of lithology-informed constraints in missing log 
reconstruction.
2.2.1. Data preparation
Effective data preparation is essential for capturing geologically 
meaningful patterns. It involves: (1) normalizing and filtering raw logs 
to reduce noise and outliers; (2) exploring lithology–log relationships 
via PCA, which provides empirical justification for embedding litho -
logical information; and (3) organizing cleaned data into training and 
validation sets for cross-well generalizability.
The dataset comprises five standard logs (GR, RHOB, NPHI, DTC, 
DTS) acquired at 0.1 m intervals, yielding 45,809 samples from six wells 
in the Volve field (depths ~2600–4500 m). These wells represent three 
lithologies—claystone, sandstone, and limestone—providing variability 
and geological context for model evaluation. Lithological classes were 
assigned from core-calibrated reports and operator facies logs, simpli -
fied into three categories following Beltran et al. (2007) to reduce 
imbalance while retaining dominant geological controls.

using min–max scaling ( Amorim et al., 2023 ) to rescale the data to a 
range of 0–1, thus ensuring the equal contribution of each well-log data
